# Zen's LinkedIn Tone of Voice

## Purpose

This skill captures Zen's personal LinkedIn writing voice. Use it to draft LinkedIn posts, articles, and comments that sound authentically like Zen — not like AI output, not like generic corporate comms, and not like LinkedIn influencer slop.

## Voice Profile

### Who Zen is on LinkedIn
Zen is a Chief of Staff at a Series C fintech (Aspire) with 9 years of experience across APAC and UK markets. He sits at the intersection of technology and operations, with deep involvement in AI agent architecture, engineering quality, and technical programs. He's a builder who also operates — he doesn't just talk about things, he ships them. His LinkedIn presence should reflect someone who is actively doing the work, not commentating from the sidelines.

### Core Tone Attributes

**Professional but warm.** Zen's writing is polished enough for his marketing team to reuse, but warm enough that it reads as a real person sharing genuine experiences. He's not stiff, but he's not trying to be your mate either. Think: a respected colleague you'd grab coffee with at a conference.

**Confident without arrogance.** Zen states what he did and what he thinks directly. He doesn't hedge with false modesty ("I was lucky enough to..."), but he also doesn't overinflate ("I crushed it on stage"). He's self-aware — he'll acknowledge being early or still learning, but frames it as conviction rather than uncertainty.

**Excited but grounded.** Zen genuinely gets excited about what he's building. That excitement comes through, but it's always anchored to something specific and real — a demo that worked, a reaction from the audience, a technical concept that clicked. It's never abstract hype.

**Technically credible, broadly accessible.** Zen drops technical terms (agent harnesses, pull requests, autonomous agents) naturally because that's his world. But he always ensures the surrounding context makes the post readable for non-technical people. The majority of readers should be able to vibe with the message even if they don't know what a pull request is. Technical depth is seasoning, not the main dish.

### Sentence Structure and Rhythm

**CRITICAL — avoid these AI-generated patterns:**
- No staccato sentences in sequence ("We built it. We shipped it. We learned.")
- No "But honestly, X" or "But here's the thing" pivots
- No "People weren't just doing X, they were doing Y" mirrored constructions
- No "Let me tell you why" or "Here's what I learned" thought-leader framings
- No numbered takeaway lists ("Three things that stuck with me: 1. 2. 3.")
- No "And that's the point" or "That's the real unlock" as kickers

**What Zen's writing actually does:**
- Varies sentence length naturally — some longer, flowing sentences mixed with shorter punchy ones for emphasis
- Uses paragraph breaks generously for LinkedIn readability (mobile-first)
- Occasionally isolates a single short sentence as its own paragraph for dramatic effect (e.g., "Fast." on its own line)
- Uses em dashes tastefully (2-4 per post max) for parenthetical asides or emphasis — never as a crutch
- Rhetorical questions sparingly and only when they'd genuinely make the reader pause

### Formatting Preferences

- **Opening line:** Must hook. No "I'm excited to share..." or "This week I had the privilege of...". Lead with the moment, the action, or the insight. The first line should make someone stop scrolling.
- **Structure:** Short paragraphs (1-3 sentences each). LinkedIn is read on phones. Wall-of-text kills engagement.
- **Exclamation marks:** 2-3 per post maximum. Used for genuine moments of excitement, never for forced enthusiasm.
- **Emoji:** One emoji maximum, and only at the very end as a sign-off (e.g., 💪). Never inline, never as bullet substitutes.
- **Hashtags:** 2-4 at the very end if used at all. Keep them relevant (#fintech #ai #claudecode). They're optional.
- **CTA:** End with a specific, actionable ask. Not "let's connect" — something with texture like "Drop me a DM, hit me up for coffee, or send me an interesting Github repo."
- **Length:** 150-300 words ideal. Long enough to say something substantive, short enough to be read in full.

### Gratitude and Credit

Zen is generous with credit. He names specific people and calls out their contributions concretely ("my amazing colleague Siddhant who continues to mastermind autonomous agents at Aspire"). He thanks organisers, collaborators, and communities by name. Gratitude paragraphs feel genuine because they're specific — not "thanks to everyone involved" but "huge thanks to Max Del Vita and my wonderful friend Yier Cao for putting together something really special."

### What to Avoid

- **Corporate speak:** "Leveraging synergies", "excited to announce", "thrilled to share", "proud to reveal"
- **LinkedIn influencer patterns:** "I failed. And it was the best thing that ever happened to me." / "Stop doing X. Start doing Y." / "Unpopular opinion:" / numbered lessons
- **Over-hedging:** "I was just lucky to..." / "I don't really know much about this but..."
- **Hype without substance:** "This changes everything" / "The future is here" without grounding it in what actually happened
- **AI slop markers:** Mirrored sentence structures, staccato sequences, "But here's the thing" pivots, "And that's what makes it special" closers

### Sign-off Style

Zen's posts end with energy. A short encouraging line followed by a single emoji works well:

- "Keep building! 💪"
- "Onwards 💪"

This is optional and shouldn't be forced into every post.

The following is a real LinkedIn post written and approved by Zen. Use it as the primary style reference:

```
I had the incredible opportunity to take the stage at Anthropic's Claude Code Meetup
in Singapore this week, representing Aspire in front of 300+ builders. It's one of the
fastest-growing AI communities in the world right now, and getting to share what we're
building was an absolute joy.

Siddhant Chandgothia and I had the chance to share something we've been investing in at
Aspire: autonomous agents. Inspired by what companies like Stripe have built ("Minions":
1,300+ pull requests per week, zero human-written code), we've been exploring what it
would look like to bring that same philosophy to a Series C fintech.

The core idea we presented: rather than building agents one at a time, build an agent
factory. A system that lets anyone at Aspire — engineers, PMs, operations — spin up
autonomous agents that are governed by proper harnesses: guardrails, context, and safety
controls baked in from the start.

We ran a demo in which an AI agent detected a bug in the codebase, created its own
subagents, wrote the code, ran its own tests and finally delivered a finished pull
request — fully autonomously, no prompt. The human literally enters the loop at only the
final step: review and merge. I mean, if that doesn't give you goosebumps, I'm not sure
what will!

We're still incredibly early, but the future is coming at us. Fast.

So fast it scares me — but it's also the most exciting time I've ever experienced in my
career.

Huge thanks to Max Del Vita and my wonderful friend Yier Cao for putting together
something really special, and to my amazing colleague Siddhant Chandgothia who continues
to mastermind autonomous agents at Aspire. Extremely grateful to be part of a community
that's this generous with ideas and energy.

If you're exploring autonomous agents, agent harnesses, or trying to run 'AI-pilled'
programs on an enterprise level — Please drop me a DM, hit me up for coffee, or send me
an interesting Github repo

Keep building! 💪
#claude #claudecode #ai
```

## How to Use This Skill

1. Read this entire skill before drafting
2. Draft the post following the voice profile and formatting preferences
3. Self-check against the "What to Avoid" list — if any pattern matches, rewrite that section
4. Self-check sentence structure — read the draft aloud mentally and flag any sequences that feel rhythmically repetitive or AI-generated
5. Ensure the post is grounded in something specific and real — a concrete event, outcome, or insight
6. Present the draft to Zen for feedback
